# Business Frameworks — NSCLC Investment Analysis

## Porter's Five Forces — NSCLC Drug Development Market

### 1. Threat of New Entrants: Moderate-Low
High barriers to entry — oncology drug development requires massive capital (PoS model shows only ~5% of Phase 1 oncology candidates reach approval), years of trials, and deep regulatory expertise. Early-phase trials are dominated by academic institutions (NCI, university cancer centers) rather than new commercial entrants — commercial entry typically happens through partnership/licensing with academia rather than de novo drug discovery.

### 2. Bargaining Power of Suppliers: Low-Moderate
"Suppliers" here mainly means clinical trial infrastructure (CROs, trial sites, patient recruitment networks) and raw biologics/manufacturing inputs. Moderately concentrated CRO market (ICON, IQVIA, etc.) gives some supplier leverage, but large pharma sponsors typically have negotiating power given trial volume.

### 3. Bargaining Power of Buyers: High
Buyers here are payers (insurers, governments) and increasingly, patients facing list prices as high as ~$279K/year (Tagrisso) to ~$54K/year (generic-displaced Tarceva). This wide price range — a 5x spread across drugs treating overlapping patient populations — signals real payer pressure and negotiating leverage, especially as biosimilars and generics erode branded pricing power (as seen with Tarceva's brand discontinuation).

### 4. Threat of Substitutes: Moderate, Rising
Within NSCLC, substitution risk comes from competing mechanisms (targeted therapy vs. immunotherapy vs. chemotherapy) and, over time, generic/biosimilar entry once patents expire. Multiple companies (AstraZeneca, Roche, BMS, Merck) each run competing late-stage assets — meaning payers and physicians have real choice among therapeutic approaches, increasing substitution pressure on any single drug.

### 5. Competitive Rivalry: High
AstraZeneca, Hoffmann-La Roche, and other major pharma companies all maintain full-pipeline presence in NSCLC simultaneously, competing across overlapping patient segments (especially EGFR+ and immunotherapy-eligible populations). High rivalry is further evidenced by trial volume concentration in Phase 2 (1,130 trials) — many competing candidates chasing similar mechanisms and patient populations.

## SWOT Analysis — AstraZeneca (NSCLC / Tagrisso)

### Strengths
- Full-pipeline presence across NSCLC development phases (62 trials spanning Early Phase 1 through Phase 4) — one of only two sponsors with this breadth, alongside Roche
- Tagrisso is a first-line standard-of-care therapy for EGFR-mutated NSCLC, with strong market positioning (65% assumed market share in SOM model)
- Established regulatory track record (2015 approval, since expanded)

### Weaknesses
- Tagrisso addresses only the EGFR-mutation-positive segment (~15% of NSCLC patients) — a narrow, biomarker-defined population limits total addressable market versus broader-mechanism competitors
- High list price (~$279K/year, the highest in the 10-drug comparison) increases payer pushback risk and exposure to pricing reform

### Opportunities
- SAM/SOM gap ($8.2B SAM vs. $3.7B SOM) suggests meaningful room to grow captured market share within the existing eligible population, through improved testing/diagnosis rates or share gains from competitors
- Potential expansion into earlier treatment lines or combination therapies, suggested by ongoing full-pipeline investment (Phase 1-4 activity)

### Threats
- High competitive rivalry (per Five Forces above) from Roche, BMS, Merck, and others in overlapping NSCLC segments
- Eventual patent expiration and biosimilar/generic entry — directly evidenced by Tarceva's brand discontinuation, a cautionary precedent within the same competitive set
- Payer pressure given the drug's premium pricing relative to competitors
